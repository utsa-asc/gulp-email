UTSA President Taylor Eighmy Shares Vision For The Future

By Jan Ross Piedad , Texas Public Radio

Six months into his presidency at the University of Texas at San Antonio, Taylor Eighmy already has ambitious plans. Working most recently in research universities like Texas Tech and the University of Tennessee, Knoxville, Eighmy has a background in civil and environmental engineering. He was an active researcher and inventor while in academia, as well as a professor and administrator.

It's not just Facebook - online quizzes also collect data about you

By Xavier Harding, Mic.com

“Any survey asking you anything, from, ‘What would you do in a certain situation?’ to your favorite color, is gleaning information about you that is useful to algorithms,” Dr. Max Kilger, the director of analytics at University of Texas at San Antonio, said in an interview. “Any quiz you take, on or off of Facebook, has a chance that the site will resell it or combine it with some existing data on you.”

S.A. congressman in vulnerable seat increases his focus on cybersecurity and artificial intelligence

By Bill Lambrecht, San Antonio Express-News

San Antonio is a main computer security hub outside of the Washington, D.C., region. It’s home to the 24th Air Force, part of the U.S. Cyber Command, a National Security Agency center and a burgeoning cybersecurity industry. Defense Department contractors have offices locally, and the University of Texas at San Antonio is among several colleges and universities offering top-ranked cybersecurity education programs.

The San Antonio Conservation Society on Wednesday evening hosted its 69th annual Historic Preservation Awards, which honors local developers, architects, craftsmen and others who have played a significant part in preserving and redeveloping some of San Antonio's most important historic structures.

Weeklong UTSA festival celebrates first-generation Roadrunners

By Staff Report, UTSA Today

To recognize the nearly half of undergraduates at The University of Texas at San Antonio (UTSA) who are the first in their families to earn a four-year college degree, UTSA is expanding its First-Gen Fest this year. ‘I Was in Danger’: What Happens When Students Harass Professors

By Bianca Quilantan, The Chronicle of Higher Education

Jody Greene thought she was unstalkable. Greene, a professor at the University of California at Santa Cruz, prided herself on the professorial boundaries she had laid out for herself and often explained their importance to her 125-student lecture classes. She was their professor, not their friend, and the classroom, she said, was a space she had marked out to be the safest place.

Leaders of a landmark study aimed at heightening the focus on family medical history and genetic drivers that put some women at higher risks than others for developing breast disease are ramping up efforts to add Black females to their hoped-for pool of 100,000 participants.

VSU Rolls Out Inclusive Gender-Identity Diversity Program

By Sierra Darville, Diverse: Issues in Higher Education

Brown Receives Record Applications, Admits 2,566

By Staff Report, Diverse: Issues in Higher Education

Brown University’s Office of College Admission last week offered admission to 2,566 students, just 7 percent of 35,438 applicants, the largest applicant pool in the school’s history. The Class of 2022 includes 1,829 offers under regular decision and 737 in the early decision program. That’s 8 percent higher than the Class of 2021, which had hit the previous high mark for applicants with 32,724.

Alabama Women Suing For Equal Pay Lack State Protection

By Staff Report, Diverse: Issues in Higher Education

Amy Heatherly believes she would have been paid at least $50,000 more to do her job over the past five years if she had been a man. As the only female human resources director overseeing compensation at the University of Alabama’s main campus, Heatherly said she knew for years she was getting paid less than three men on a similar management level with fewer years of experience.
